Glaucoma

Definition of glaucoma

Glaucoma is a leading cause of blindness for people over 60 years old But blindness from glaucoma can often be prevented with early treatment In a healthy eye, fluid leaves the eye through the drainage angle, keeping pressure stable.

Symptoms

    Glaucoma is a progressive eye disease that can lead to vision loss and blindness if left untreated. In its early stages, glaucoma often has no symptoms, which is why regular eye exams are important for early detection. However, as the disease progresses, some common symptoms may
    include:
    Peripheral vision loss: Glaucoma typically affects the peripheral or side vision first, making it difficult to see objects in your peripheral field of view.
    Tunnel vision: As glaucoma progresses, it can cause a narrowing of your visual field until you have tunnel vision, where you can only see objects directly in front of you.
    Halos around lights: Glaucoma can cause the pupil of your eye to dilate more than usual, which can result in
    seeing halos or rings around bright lights.
    Eye pain: In some cases, glaucoma can cause eye pain or discomfort, particularly in the morning or after waking up from sleep.
    Nausea and vomiting: In advanced cases of glaucoma, increased pressure within the eye can cause nausea and vomiting.
    It's important to note that these symptoms may also indicate other eye conditions, so it's essential to have regular eye exams with an ophthalmologist to diagnose and manage any eye health concerns.

Causes

    Glaucoma is a complex eye disease that can have multiple causes. Here are some of the most common causes of glaucoma:
    intraocular pressure (IOP):
    This is the most common cause of glaucoma, where the fluid inside the eye (aqueous humor) builds up, causing an increase in IOP. Over time, this can damage the optic nerve and lead to vision loss.
    Optic nerve damage: Glaucoma can also occur due to damage to the optic nerve, which is responsible for transmitting visual information from the eye to the brain. This can be caused by factors such as genetics, aging, or injury.
    Low tension glaucoma (LTG): This type of glaucoma occurs when there is optic nerve damage and vision loss, despite normal IOP levels. The exact cause of LTG is not fully understood.
    Secondary glaucoma: This type of glaucoma occurs as a result of another eye condition or injury, such as cataract surgery, trauma, or inflammation.
    Congenital glaucoma: This is a rare form of glaucoma that is present at birth or develops during childhood due to abnormalities in the eye's drainage system.
    It's important to note that some people may be at higher risk for developing glaucoma due to factors such as age, family history, ethnicity, and certain medical conditions. Regular eye exams with an ophthalmologist can help identify any signs of glaucoma and manage the condition before it causes significant vision loss.

Treatment

    The treatment for glaucoma depends on the type and severity of the disease.
    Here are some common treatments for glaucoma:
    Medications: Eye drops, pills, or ointments are commonly prescribed to lower IOP levels. These medications work by either reducing the production of aqueous humor or increasing its drainage from the eye.
    Laser therapy: Laser treatment, such as selective laser trabeculoplasty (SLT) or laser iridotomy, can help improve drainage of aqueous humor and lower IOP levels.
    Surgery: In some cases, surgery may be necessary to manage glaucoma. Common surgical procedures include trabeculectomy, which involves creating a new drainage pathway for aqueous humor, and implantation of a drainage device, such as a shunt or tube, to help drain excess fluid from the eye.
    Lifestyle changes: Certain lifestyle changes, such as maintaining a healthy weight, exercising regularly, and protecting your eyes from injury, can help manage glaucoma and prevent further damage to the optic nerve.
    It's important to note that regular follow-up appointments with an ophthalmologist are essential to monitor the progression of glaucoma and adjust treatment plans as needed. Additionally, it's crucial to adhere to any prescribed medications or follow-up appointments to prevent further vision loss.

Optic nerve: the optic nerve appears normal and has a round shape. the optic nerve can become damaged, causing it to appear thinner or more cupped in shape.
Intraocular pressure (IOP): IOP levels are within a normal range. IOP levels can be too high, leading to damage to the optic nerve and vision loss.

Nerve fiber layer: this layer appears normal and uniform. this layer can become thin or irregular due to damage to the optic nerve, which can be seen on an optical coherence tomography (OCT) scan.
Cornea: the cornea appears clear and smooth. the cornea can become thinner or more curved due to changes in IOP levels or other factors, which can affect vision and make it more difficult to manage glaucoma with medications or surgery.
